    # METABOLIC EMERGENCIES

    # Tumor lysis Syndrome and Hypercalcemia

    # 林協霆

    # TUMOR Lysis syndrome

    # Definition

    TLS is an oncologic emergency caused by electrolyte abnormalities

    • a/w spontaneous and/or tx-induced cell death that can be potentially fatal.
    • There are 2 main classifications,
      • lab
      • clinical

    # 4 key electrolyte abnormalities 2° to excessive tumor/cell lysis:

    1. Hyperkalemia
    2. Hyperphosphatemia
    3. Hypocalcemia
    4. Hyperuricemia (2° to catabolism of nucleic acids)

    # Presentation

    上吐下瀉、不尿不便、神經學

    • nausea
    • vomiting
    • diarrhea
    • constipation
    • oliguria/low UOP, wt gain, ARF,
    • weakness cramps, seizures, tetany, or dysrhythmias

    # Cairo–Bishop Classification System for TLS (Br J Haematol 2004;127:3)

    # Lab TLS:

    • Abnormality in ≥2 w/in 3 d before or 7 d after the initiation of cytotoxic Rx
      • UA >8 mg/dL or 25% increase from baseline
      • Potassium >6 mEq/L or 25% increase from baseline
      • Phosphate >4.5 mg/dL or 25% increase from baseline
      • Ca <7 mg/dL or 25% decrease from baseline

    # Clinical TLS: ≥1 lab TLS marker plus ≥1 clinical abnormalities

    • Increased serum Cr (≥1.5× ULN)
    • Cardiac dysrhythmia
    • Seizure

    # A severity grading system for TLS

    has been established (on a scale of 0–5) based on the degree of Cr increase, presence & severity of cardiac dysrhythmia, & the presence & severity of seizures

    # Common Malignancies Associated with a High Risk of Developing TLS in Adult Patients

    • Acute leukemias (AML, ALL) w/large leukemic phase (>25–50K WBC)
    • High-grade lymphomas such as
      • Burkitt lymphoma
      • DLBCL
        • (of note, indolent lymphomas rarely cause TLS unless a large leukemic phase is present)

    # Certain targeted therapies have a higher risk of TLS in CLL or indolent NHL:

    # Venetoclax, especially w/

    • CYP3A (grapefruit juice)
    • P-gp inhibitors (amiodarone, clarithromycin, cyclosporine, diltiazem, erythromycin, ketoconazole, itraconazole, propafenone, quinidine, and verapamil.)

    # Obinutuzumab, no large TLS risk w/ follicular lymphoma

    # Risk Factors

    (Br J Haematol 2010;149:578; N Engl J Med 2011;364:1844)

    # disease characteristics a/w TLS risk:

    • Rapidly progressive
    • chemosensitive
    • myelo- or lymphoproliferative disease, especially w/large leukemic phase (>25–50K WBC),
    • bulky disease (>10 cm in diameter),
    • extensive organ/marrow infiltration.

    # Pt characteristics a/w TLS risk:

    • Baseline impaired renal function,
      • oliguria, exposure to nephrotoxins, hyperuricemia
    • Volume depletion/inadequate hydration, acidic urine

    # Monitoring

    UOP with advice goal of ≥100 mL/h

    # Telemetry

    # Labs:

    BMP, UA, phosphate, Ca, & LDH

    # High-risk pts

    could be monitored in ICU w/frequent labs

    # Intermediate- or low-risk pts

    require lab monitoring over 8–12 h & daily, respectively

    # Prophylaxis

    (DeVita, Hellman, and Rosenberg’s Cancer: Principles and Practice of Oncology. 10th ed. Lippincott Williams and Wilkins, 2015; Br J Haematol 2011;154:3)

    • IV hydration: Crystalloid solution (eg, NS) at 2–3 L/m2 per d, ± diuretics
    • Allopurinol and/or rasburicase Rx (rasburicase [3–6 mg IV] in high-risk pts only), 1-time dosing + allopurinol vs. daily dosing.
      • 單次6mg足矣
      • NB: Febuxostat has no established role in TLS PPx
    • Urinary alkalinization no longer considered standard, not needed w/rasburicase

    # Management

    # Hyperkalemia:

    # Clinical S/S:

    • Asymptomatic
    • cardiac dysrhythmia and/or ECG changes
      • (peaked T waves > PR seg prolong > p waves disappear > QRS prolonged > sine wave > asystole),
    • muscle cramps, paresthesias
    • N/V, diarrhea

    # Tx:

    # Mod. & asx, >6 mmol/L:

    • Cation exchange resins (Na polystyrene sulfonate/Kayexalate),
    • avoid IV & oral potassium,
    • stop meds (ACEi/ARBs/K+ sparing diuretics/NSAIDs, etc)

    # Sev. (>7 mmol/L) and/or symptomatic:

    As above +

    • Ca gluconate 1–2 g 本院400mg一支 : 4支 for life-threatening arrhythmias q5min prn,
    • regular insulin (5–10 U IV) + D50 (25 g of glucose) IV,
    • Na bicarb (50–100 mEq) 本院16.6mEq/支 : 4支
    • loop diuretics
    • inhaled β-agonists (albuterol in higher doses 10–20 mg) 本院5mg/支 :2到4支
    • in sev./refractory cases: hemodialysis.

    # Hyperphosphatemia:

    # Clinical s/s:

    • Asymptomatic,
    • acute renal failure,
    • 2° hypocalcemia (phosCa product >70),

    NB: Hyperphosphatemia in the presence of hypercalcemia imposes a high risk of metastatic calcification

    # Tx:

    • Exogenous sources of phosphate (enteral/parenteral nutrition, medications) should be reduced or eliminated.
    • PO phosphate binders
      • calcium carbonate, calcium acetate,
      • sevelamer carbonate, lanthanum carbonate
        • in pts with hypercalcemia;
    • in sev./refractory cases, hemodialysis may be beneficial

    # Hypocalcemia:

    # Clinical s/s:

    • excitation of nerve & muscle cells,
      • causing neuromuscular irritability/spasm (eg, tetany, paresthesias, muscle twitching/cramping, laryngospasm/bronchospasm)
      • cardiac dysfunction (eg, dysrhythmia, HF),
      • mental status changes (eg, confusion, delirium, & hallucinations)
      • potential seizures, sudden death

    # Tx:

    • Confirm Ca2+, ionized Ca2+, albumin, PTH, vitamin D3, & Mg levels, & BMP
    • Correct hyperphosphatemia & in symptomatic cases give IV Ca gluconate (preferred to Ca chloride), 10–15 mg of calcium per kilogram body wt in 1 L of D5W & infused over 4–6 h, w/goal serum calcium level 7–8.5 mg/dL

    # Hyperuricemia:

    • Clinical s/s: Asymptomatic, ARF

    # Tx:

    • Allopurinol vs. rasburicase, hemodialysis reserved for sev/refractory cases
    • No role for febuxostat at this time

    # Allopurinol vs. Rasburicase

    # Hypercalcemia

    # Hypercalcemia: Overview

    # Epidemiology:

    ~10–30% of all pts w/malignancy

    # Definition:

    • Normal values: 9–10.5 mg/dL,
    • mild = elevated but <12;
    • mod. = 12–14;
    • sev. >14

    # 一般情況都是抽Ca+Alb:

    Corrected [Ca] = Measured total [Ca] + (0.8 x (4.0 - [Alb])) (這個單位是mg/dL，速記：如果Albumin是2.5，要加2)

    # FreeCa，一般來說是一般Ca的40-50%

    和信FreeCa單位是mg/dL，所以直接乘起來沒問題，但是如果今天是在北榮的話，單位是mmol/L，就是要除分子量，就是要再除四

    # TLDR

    如果Ca = 8 mg/dL Albumin = 4 則 iCa = 4 mg/dL, 1mmol/L

    Ca (mmol/L) = Ca measured (mmol/L) + 0.02*[40 - albumin (g/L]) (note: may be inaccurate in renal dx/ESRD pts)

    # Pathogenesis:

    1. Humorally mediated via PTHrP (secreted from tumor cells)
    2. osteolytic focal bone destruction from paracrine factors,
    3. rarely ectopic calcitriol (lymphoma), 1,25-vit D or PTH production

    # Symptoms:

    • Polyuria/polydipsia
    • intravascular volume depletion,
    • AKI
    • lethargy, AMS, rarely coma/seizures
    • N/V
    • ECG changes (↓ QTc)
    • ↑ risk of digoxin tox

    # Workup:

    # H/P:

    • Bone pain, meds/diet (thiazides/Li, vitamin supplements, TPN volume status, FHH)

    # Labs:

    • CMP (Ca, Cr, alb, phos), iCa, iPTH,
    • if unclear source → PTHrP & vit D (1,25 & 25-OH),
    • still unclear → SPEP/UPEP/FLC,
    • still unclear → CXR/vit A/endocrine workup

    # Hypercalcemia: Humoral Mechanisms

    # Epidemiology:

    • ~80% of all hyperCa of malignancy

    # Pathogenesis:

    • PTHrP (shares same amino acid terminus as PTH)
    • less commonly calcitriol are secreted by tumor → ↑ bone turnover & ↑ renal Ca reabsorption.

    # Common Tumors:

    • SCC of lung
    • head/neck
    • renal
    • bladder
    • NHL
    • leukemias
    • HL (calcitriol)

    # Hypercalcemia: Osteolytic Bone Lesions

    # Epidemiology:

    ~20% of all hyperCa of malignancy

    # Pathogenesis:

    Tumor mets to bone → express PTHrP locally (paracrine) → activate osteoclasts via RANKL–RANK interaction → bone resorption → ↑Ca

    # Common Tumors:

    Breast, lung, MM, renal, less likely prostate (osteoblastic)

    # Treatment

    • Medication, supplement, & diet review; stop offending agents, if possible
    • Tx underlying malignancy, only way to effectively treat, all other tx are temporizing
    • IVFs are essential in tx → rapidly correct hypovolemia & AKI → promote calciuresis

    # Osteoclast inhibition: Modern cornerstone of tx w/IVFs

    # Bisphosphonates:

    Pamidronate (60–90 mg IV) & zoledronic acid (4 mg IV), which may be superior to pamidronate (JCO 2001;19:558), either acceptable; ZA ↑ risk of AKI/ATN. Both ↑ risk of ↓Ca.

    # RANK-L inhibitor:

    Denosumab approved for hyperCa of malignancy refractory to bisphosphonates, ↑ risk of hypoCa, especially if recent bisphosphonate use or prostate CA w/blastic lesions

    # Loop diuretics:

    Unnecessary unless concern for volume overload; no data support utility (Ann Int Med 2008;149:259).

    # Calcitonin:

    Rapid, temporary ↓ Ca, monitor for hypoCa, HSR, & tachyphylaxis (>48 h)

    # HD:

    If all above fail → AKI progresses → sev. hypervolemia
