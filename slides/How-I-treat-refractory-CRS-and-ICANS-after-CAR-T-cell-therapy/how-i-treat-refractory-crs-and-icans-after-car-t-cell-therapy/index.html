    # Introduction

    • CAR T-cell therapy is potent, but toxicity mitigation is necessary
    • definition and grading systems for CRS and immune effector cell–associated neurotoxicity syndrome (ICANS) have been developed.
    • Severe toxicities, including refractoriness to standard management with tocilizumab and steroids, still occur.

    # Tocilizumab and Corticosteroids for CRS and ICANS

    • Tocilizumab and corticosteroids were initially used for severe CRS and/or ICANS.
    • Now, these therapies are increasingly used at earlier stages to reduce the incidence of severe toxicity.
      • Pre-emptive tocilizumab and/or steroids with lower grade toxicity can prevent more severe manifestations.
      • Prophylactic treatment before any toxicity is observed is also being explored.
    • Refractory CRS and/or ICANS are defined as persistent or progressive disease despite optimal use of tocilizumab and/or steroids.
    • Available strategies have shifted towards prevention of ≥grade 3 CRS and ICANS.

    # Workup or evaluation and supportive care recommendations (all grades)

    • CBC, CMP, Mg, Phos, CRP, LDH, uric acid, fibrinogen, PT/PTT, and ferritin
    • blood and urine cultures, CXR
    • If neutropenic, follow institutional neutropenic fever guidelines
    • If grade 2 or higher CRS (eg, shock or hypoxia)
      • should be monitored with continuous cardiac telemetry and pulse oximetry.
    • severe CRS, consider performing an echocardiogram to assess cardiac function.
    • Perform cardiac monitoring in patients who experience at least G2 CRS, clinically significant arrhythmia, and additionally as clinically indicated
    • Consider screening for CMV and EBV
    • Consider chest or abdominal CT imaging, brain MRI, and/or lumbar puncture.

    Santomasso BD, Nastoupil LJ, Adkins S, et al. Management of Immune-Related Adverse Events in Patients Treated With Chimeric Antigen Receptor T-Cell Therapy: ASCO Guideline. J Clin Oncol. 2021;39(35):3978-3992. doi:10.1200/JCO.21.01992

    # Cases

    # Case 1: Refractory CRS in a DLBCL Patient

    • A 59-year-old male
    • DLBCL received CD19 CAR T-cell therapy after 3 prior lines of therapy.
    • On day 0, he developed grade 1 CRS and progressed to grade 2 CRS with hypoxia by day 3.
    • Tocilizumab at 8 mg/kg was administered, but he required a second dose and dexamethasone, 10 mg IV every 6 hours, because of persistent fevers and worsening hypoxia.
    • By day 4, ferritin x3 within 24 hours to 23,000 ng/mL, → ICU for high-flow oxygen and vasopressors.
    • DIC, thrombocytopenia, and AKI creatinine ≥3 mg/dL.
    • The corticosteroid dose was increased to methylprednisolone 1 g IV daily.

    :::half

    • Patients with high tumor burden and primary refractory disease are
      • at high risk of both CRS and lymphoma relapse.
    • CD19 CAR T-cell products incorporating 4-1BB (ie, tisagenlecleucel) have lower toxicity rates than those costimulated by CD28 (axi-cel), but severe CRS has been reported for all products.

    !!! info tisa-cel lower toxicity than axi-cel

    • With persistent grade 2 CRS despite repeat doses of tocilizumab and/or single dose corticosteroids, or progression to grade 3 CRS,
      • dexamethasone is often escalated up to 10 mg IV every 6 hours.
      • methylprednisolone 1 g IV per day and/or initiation of alternative CRS-directed therapies.
    • Once a patient has received 2 doses of tocilizumab
      • repeat doses are generally not recommended.
    • Consideration of both alternative etiologies and incorporation of additional therapies is imperative for refractory CRS.

    # Case 1: Management of Refractory CRS in a DLBCL Patient

    • Concurrent infections, including bacteremia and CMV viremia, were identified in the patient with refractory CRS.
      • Antibiotics and CMV treatment were initiated.
    • Early restaging via abdominal CT scan showed
      • stabilization of the abdominal mass.
    • Anakinra 100 mg IV every 6 hours was started on the afternoon of day 4 for refractory CRS.
    • The patient’s condition subsequently improved, and anakinra and corticosteroid doses were tapered over 1 week.
    • He experienced a maximum ICANS of grade 1.
    • His day 30 positron emission tomography/CT scan showed a complete metabolic remission

    # Anakinra

    • Disease-modifying Antirheumatic Drugs
    • Recombinant human interleukin-1 (IL-1) receptor antagonist
    • Administer sub-Q, 100mg
    • Uses: Rheumatoid Arthritis, Cryopyrin-associated Periodic Syndromes, Deficiency of Interleukin-1 Receptor Antagonist
    • Warnings:
      • Opportunistic infections (fungal, mycobacterial, bacterial)
      • Concomitant use of TNF blocking agents (e.g., adalimumab, etanercept, infliximab) not recommended.
      • Neutropenia

    # Differential Diagnosis for Early Clinical Deterioration after CAR T-cell Therapy

    • Top differential diagnoses include:
      • refractory CRS, infection, and cancer progression.
    • Infectious workup includes standard cultures performed at first fever and every 24 to 48 hours while the patient is persistently febrile.
      • CMV/adenoviremia and other viremias,
      • nasopharyngeal viral testing for respiratory infections
    • The patient had no evidence of gastrointestinal perforation or ischemia, which can occur among patients with abdominal lymphoma.

    # Management of Refractory CRS

    • For CRS refractory to high doses of corticosteroids:
      • anakinra often considered.
    • Once a clinical response is achieved, anakinra and corticosteroids:
      • tapered as indicated, prolonged use at high doses is not indicated.
    • Extra vigilance and enhanced antimicrobial prophylaxis is warranted,
    • Cytopenias are a complication of, and can further complicate, refractory CRS

    !!! Warning prolonged use at high doses is not indicated

    # Case 2: CD19 CAR T-cell Therapy for Refractory Follicular Lymphoma Transformed to DLBCL

    • A 71-year-old woman
    • follicular lymphoma transformed to DLBCL was referred for receipt of CD19 CAR T cells for treatment of progressive nodal and extranodal disease refractory to 3 prior lines of therapy.
    • She was not given any bridging therapy before axicabtagene ciloleucel.
    • On day 2, she developed grade 1 CRS, with persistent fever until day 5, when she was given dexamethasone 10 mg IV once.
    • On day 6, she presented with grade 2 CRS and grade 1 ICANS characterized by fever, hypoxia, and dysphasia.
    • She received tocilizumab 8 mg/kg and dexamethasone 10 mg IV once for the ICANS.

    # Early Intervention and Corticosteroid Use in CAR T-cell Therapy

    • The comparison of cohorts 1 and 2 with cohort 4 found that the median overall corticosteroid exposure declined from 6886 mg to 939 mg with earlier use of corticosteroids.
    • Overall, the trend in clinical practice is toward intervening earlier at lower grades of CRS and ICANS.
    • Age alone is not a driving factor for toxicity severity, as shown by findings from studies such as Ram et al’s comparison of geriatric and younger patients.

    !!! Info Earlier intervention with corticosteroids may help to limit a more prolonged course of corticosteroids.

    # Early Intervention for Patients at High Risk of Toxicity

    • In patients who may not tolerate prolonged low-grade CRS (such as elderly patients or those who are frail) or patients who are at high risk of subsequent progression to severe toxicities, early intervention with tocilizumab and/or corticosteroids during lower grade toxicities is recommended.
    • Although results of studies incorporating prophylactic corticosteroids are promising, additional studies are needed before systematically incorporating the prophylactic use of steroids for risk mitigation.

    # Management of Grade 3 ICANS in a Patient Receiving CAR T-cell Therapy

    • On day 8, the patient showed global aphasia and epileptiform activity upon an EEG, consistent with grade 3 ICANS,
      • despite concurrent improvement in CRS to grade 1
    • For treatment of ICANS, she received methylprednisolone 1 gm IV daily for 2 days followed by a dexamethasone taper to discontinuation over 2 weeks.
    • She also received anakinra 100 mg IV every 6 hours, which was tapered over a 10-day course.
    • Seizure prophylaxis with levetiracetam (500 mg daily) was increased to therapeutic doses and 2 additional antiepileptics, lacosamide and phenytoin, were initiated to control seizure activity based on neurology consultation.

    # Use of Corticosteroids in Management of CRS and ICANS

    • Corticosteroids are generally accepted for refractory CRS and as first-line therapy for ICANS.
    • Dexamethasone is frequently recommended for ICANS because it crosses the blood-brain barrier.
    • Package inserts for US FDA-approved CD19 CAR T-cell products recommend dexamethasone or methylprednisolone for the treatment of severe ICANS.
    • There are conflicting data on the impact that corticosteroid exposure has on CAR T-cell efficacy, with some studies noting no change in the efficacy, whereas others show decreased efficacy.
    • The use of intrathecal corticosteroids has also been reported for use in severe or refractory ICANS, yet its use as a systemic steroid-sparing therapy warrants prospective study.

    # Case 3: Use of CD19 CAR T-cell Therapy in Down Syndrome and rB-ALL

    • A 15-year-old male with constitutional trisomy 21 and
      • relapsed B-cell acute lymphoblastic leukemia (B-ALL)
      • presented with 30% bone marrow blasts with low-level circulating peripheral blasts.
    • The patient received LD chemotherapy and CAR T-cell infusion for relapsed B-ALL with 30% marrow involvement after bridging chemotherapy.
    • He was on physiologic hydrocortisone for adrenal insufficiency.
    • He developed fever, representing grade 1 CRS, on day 5 and was initially responsive to supportive measures.
    • However, he developed more persistent fevers, hypotension, rising creatinine, and decreasing urine output (grade 2 CRS) over 3 days.
    • He was given tocilizumab 8 mg/kg for persistent grade 2 CRS to prevent grade 3 CRS.

    # Management of Adrenal Insufficiency and CRS Mitigation in CD19 CAR T-cell Therapy

    • For patients with established or suspected adrenal insufficiency, maintenance of physiologic dosing with escalation to stress-dosing during CRS should be considered to maintain hemodynamic stability and offset the need for higher doses of corticosteroids.
    • Consistent with preemptive strategies tested by Gardner et al, tocilizumab was given for persistent symptoms of mild CRS and before the development of life-threatening toxicities (eg, grade 3 CRS).
    • The approach of preventing more severe CRS/ICANS in children and young adults may be a unique consideration of younger patients in which baseline comorbidities may be less severe, and patients may be able to better tolerate grade 2 CRS/ICANS without more severe complications.
    • In patients with DS: monitor congenital cardiac complications.

    # Development and Management of HLH-Like Complications after CD19 CAR T-cell Therapy

    • Approximately 10 days after tocilizumab, the patient developed a rapidly rising ferritin level (>100 000 μg/L) and hepatic transaminitis (>10 × upper limit of normal [ULN]), disproportional to C-reactive protein, which was decreasing.
    • The patient also developed severe hypofibrinogenemia (<100 mg/dL)
    • Despite stable vital signs, laboratory parameters worsened, prompting the initiation of methylprednisolone (2 mg/kg per day) and anakinra (8 mg/kg per day) to treat HLH-like complications.
    • After a short course of steroids and 1 week of anakinra with tapering doses, laboratory values normalized.
    • The patient achieved a minimal residual disease-negative complete response.

    # HLH-Like Toxicities as a Potential Complication of CAR T-cell Therapy

    • Recently termed as immune effector cell-associated HLH-like syndrome (IEC-HS) through an American Society for Transplantation and Cellular Therapy (ASTCT) effort, this hyperinflammatory syndrome manifests with cytopenia, hepatic dysfunction, hypofibrinogenemia, and hyperferritinemia.
    • Because both primary/familial and secondary forms of HLH are pathologically linked to T-cell activation and immune dysregulation, this complication can be particularly challenging to diagnose with underlying hematologic malignancies.
    • Initially described as part of severe CRS, more recent reports have described delayed manifestations of HLH-like toxicities even with low-grade CRS, particularly in patients receiving CD22 CAR T cells.
    • In BCMA-targeting CAR T-cell therapies, in which severe CRS is less frequent, HLH-or macrophage activation syndrome-like toxicities are also becoming more apparent and are listed as potential toxicities on the FDA-approved package inserts.

    # Management of HLH-Like Toxicities in CD19 CAR T-cell Therapy

    • In case 3, anakinra and corticosteroids were used as first-line strategies based on their established role in the treatment of primary or secondary HLH.
    • This approach has been recently endorsed by the ASTCT Committee on Cellular Therapy, although further study is needed.
    • Tocilizumab was not indicated in case 3, as the patient’s clinical presentation and stable vital signs did not meet the standard criteria for CRS.
    • Multicenter efforts are ongoing to establish clear criteria to improve understanding of IEC-HS and formulate optimal treatment approaches, particularly for refractory cases.
    • Therapeutic strategies for refractory IEC-HS are under study and include ruxolitinib, emapalumab, and etoposide.




    # Prevention of severe and/or refractory CRS/ICANS

    # Key Points

    • Distinguishing toxicities that respond to first-line approaches from those requiring additional investigations or interventions is a critical consideration.
    • Early identification and preemptive interventions in high risk pt
    • Risk-mitigation preventive strategies for those at high risk of toxicity may improve outcomes.

    # Approach to mitigate severe CRS/ICANS and progressive toxicities despite the optimal use of first-line therapies

    # Disease type and biology

    • Highly proliferative aggressive diseases, are at higher risk of severe CRS/ICANS than diseases,
      • DLBCL and B-ALL, follicular lymphoma, or myeloma.
    • marginal zone lymphoma has a higher rate of CRS/ICANS than follicular lymphoma, and transformed marginal zone lymphoma may be at higher risk than transformed follicular lymphoma or de novo DLBCL.
    • severe and refractory CRS and ICANS may occur in any disease type,
      • especially among heavily pretreated and refractory patients.
    • Management recommendations for cases of severe and refractory CRS or ICANS are generalizable across disease types.

    # Disease burden

    • higher inflammatory cytokine levels and CAR T-cell expansion.
      • increased risk of relapse after CAR T-cell therapy, even though remission rates are often comparable.
    • severe CRS are at a higher risk for CAR T-cell failure.
    • Debunking patients before CAR T-cell therapy can be tried, but patients receive CAR T-cell therapy because they are resistant to other treatments.
    • Debulking radiation, however, the optimal timing, dose, and field for bridging radiation therapy are unknown.

    !!! Warning rapidly progressive disease, high tumor burdens, or poor performance status may be better candidates for therapies other than CAR T cells.

    # CAR T-cell construct

    • CD28-costimulated CAR T-cell products demonstrate higher rates of CRS and ICANS compared to 4-1BB-costimulated products in DLBCL with CD19 CAR T cells.
    • ICANS may be more severe when targeting CD19, potentially because of the on-target, off-tumor targeting of CD19 on brain mural cells.
    • Unique movement-related toxicities have been observed when targeting BCMA, possibly because of off-tumor expression of these targets in and around the brain.
    • Even when the target and costimulatory domain are identical, kinetics of toxicity may differ between products, affecting clinical management.

    !!! info When multiple CAR T-cell products are available for a given disease, consideration of a product with potentially lower toxicity

    # Pharmacologic interventions for severe and/or refractory toxicities

    • Evidence-based data on the use of pharmacologic interventions for treatment of severe and/or refractory toxicities in CD19 CAR T-cell therapy are generally lacking.
    • Limited data is available from single-institutional experiences or case reports, preclinical data, T-cell–directed mechanisms of action, or adopted for use based on efficacy in alternative hyperinflammatory settings.
    • Risk of infection with additive use of immunosuppressive agents is a concern.
    • Consultation with infectious disease specialists is suggested to optimize infection surveillance strategies and use of prophylactic/preemptive antimicrobials.

    # Anti-Cytokine Directed

    # Anakinra

    • IL-1 receptor antagonist.
      • reduce signs/symptoms and slow the progression of damage in
        • adults with moderately to severely active RA who have failed ≥1 DMARDs, or
        • neonatal-onset multisystem inflammatory disorder.
      • Administered by IV or SC, with preference for IV administration in patients with edema in whom SC administration may not be as reliable.

    # Siltuximab

    • IL-6 antagonist.
      • for multicentric Castleman disease who are HIV- and HHV-8–negative.
      • limited to 2L or rCRS/ICANS after the use of multiple other agents.

    # Emapalumab

    • IFN-γ–blocking antibody.
    • Used for the treatment of adult and pediatric patients with primary HLH with refractory, recurrent, or progressive disease or intolerance to conventional HLH therapy.
    • Clinical experience is limited.

    # T-Cell Targeted

    # Antithymocyte globulin (ATG)

    • Direct T-cell targeting.
    • Used for prophylaxis and treatment of acute rejection in patients receiving a kidney transplant, or use in conjunction with concomitant immunosuppression.
    • Potential use is based on clinical efficacy of targeting T cells.
    • Data on CRS/ICANS are limited.
    • Risk of infection/immunosuppression is high.

    # Alemtuzumab (anti-CD52)

    • Depletion of T and B cells by binding to CD52 on the cell surface.
    • Used for treatment of patients with relapsing forms of MS.
    • Emerging use to facilitate engraftment of allogeneic or off-the-shelf CAR T cells.

    # Cyclophosphamide

    • Alkylating agent targeting T cells.
    • Multiple indications for use in pediatrics and adults with malignancies and minimal change nephrotic syndrome.
    • Could be used for eradicating T cells.
    • Limited experience (single case report) in the use for refractory CRS/ICANS.

    # TKIs

    # Dasatinib: TKI (BCR-ABL)

    • Used for adults in chronic, accelerated, or blast phase of Ph+ CML; for adults with Ph+ ALL.
    • Preclinical studies demonstrate the ability of dasatinib to suppress CAR T-cell cytotoxicity, cytokine secretion, and proliferation.

    # Ibrutinib

    • BTK inhibitor.
    • Used for adults with MCL who have received at least 1 prior therapy, for CLL with 17p deletion, or in those who have received at least 1 prior therapy or who have Waldenstrom macroglobulinemia.
    • Emerging clinical data incorporating ibrutinib suggest the potential of reducing CRS severity.

    # Ruxolitinib or alternative JAK1 inhibitors

    • JAK inhibitor.
    • Used for the treatment of adults with myelofibrosis and polycythemia vera, and for the treatment of adults and pediatric patients aged >12 y with steroid refractory acute GVHD or chronic GVHD after failure of >1-2 lines of systemic therapy.
    • Preclinical studies demonstrate a role of JAK pathway singling blockade and dose-dependent reduction of multiple cytokines implicated in CRS.

    # CAR T-Cell Construct-Based Safety Switches in CD19 CAR T-cell Therapy

    • CAR T-cell constructs can incorporate suicide switches or alternative transcriptional controls
    • Suicide switches can be targeted by the synthetic dimerizing drug rimiducid
    • Truncated-targetable receptors, such as EGFR or CD20, can be targeted by monoclonal antibodies like cetuximab or rituximab
    • These agents can be used to eradicate CAR T cells in cases of refractory disease or life-threatening CAR T-cell–mediated toxicities.

    # Anti-CAR T-cell-directed strategies

    • When patients have severe or refractory CRS and/or ICANS, targeting the remaining CAR T cells is often the last resort.
    • Anti-CAR T-cell–directed interventions may include chemotherapy, T-cell–directed therapies, tyrosine kinase inhibitors, or CAR construct–directed therapies.
    • Experience with these interventions is limited, and infection risk may be particularly high, especially when given after multiple prior immunosuppressive approaches.
    • Further studies are needed to evaluate the efficacy and safety of these interventions for the treatment of severe or refractory CRS/ICANS.

    # Supportive Care Measures for Management of Severe or Refractory Toxicities in CD19 CAR T-cell Therapy

    • management of:
      • cytopenias
      • infections
      • hypotension
      • coagulopathy
      • electrolyte disturbances
    • Early intervention and management of adverse effects may improve patient outcomes and reduce the risk of long-term complications.

    # Supportive Care Measures for Patients with Severe or Refractory CRS or ICANS in CD19 CAR T-cell Therapy

    # Neurology

    • Identify patients at high risk of developing ICANS and engage neurology early.
    • Tailor duration of steroids in patients with severe ICANS to treat toxicity but limit adverse side effects.

    # Immune/Hematologic

    • Limit the duration of high-dose steroids for the management of toxicity to diminish immune suppression.
    • Use cytokine-directed treatments, such as anakinra, as a steroid-sparing approach.
    • Optimize approaches to treat ongoing cytopenias.

    # Infectious Disease

    • trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole, for Pneumocystis jirovecii prophylaxis.
    • Ppx acyclovir or valacyclovir, for herpes virus prophylaxis before conditioning chemotherapy.
    • broad-spectrum antibiotics and blood cultures for patients with neutropenic fever.
    • Assess for CMV, adeno- or other viremias in patients with persistent cytopenia after treatment.
    • antifungal prophylaxis in patients with prolonged immunosuppression due to CRS/ICANS management and cytopenias.

    # Rehabilitation

    • Involve inpatient rehabilitation services for patients, particularly those with lengthy hospital stays or those who received prolonged steroids, after they are clinically stable.
    • Encourage engagement of caregivers and social workers.

    # Cognitive/Psychosocial

    • Consult psychiatry or psychiatric oncology to aid in the management of delirium, particularly in older or pediatric patients who develop ICANS.

    # Medical

    • Patients with severe or refractory CRS and/or ICANS typically receive prolonged courses of high-dose corticosteroids with other immunosuppressive agents.
    • To mitigate the deleterious effects of corticosteroids, the use of steroid-sparing, cytokine-directed medications for prophylaxis, or treatment of CAR-mediated toxicity should be assessed.
    • Infectious complications are frequent after CAR T-cell therapy, and severe CRS and prolonged corticosteroid exposure are risk factors for severe infections.
    • Prophylactic antimicrobials are essential to reduce infections, including emergence of latent and opportunistic infections.
    • Exposure to broad-spectrum antibiotics targeting obligate anaerobes within the 4 weeks before CAR T-cell therapy has been associated with worse outcomes, possibly because of alterations in the intestinal microbiome.
    • The impact of antibiotics administered after CAR T-cell therapy is not yet known.

    # Rehabilitation and cognitive and psychosocial considerations

    • Multiple factors, including CRS and ICANS, immobility, malnutrition, infection, and the catabolic effects of prolonged corticosteroids, may lead to a deterioration in the conditioning and mental status
    • Early considerations for rehabilitation to address deconditioning and steroid myopathy that may result in proximal limb weakness is needed
    • Emerging research suggests the presence of short- and long-term biobehavioral effects of CAR T-cell therapy, but more work is needed in this area to understand the long-term implications for patients who experience severe or refractory CAR-mediated toxicities.

    # Conclusions

    # Take Home Messages

    • Prophylaxis and early treatment of CRS appear to play a role in preventing subsequent severe toxicity in CD19 CAR T-cell therapy.
    • Rates of severe CRS and ICANS may depend on histology, tumor burden, and CAR T-cell product type, making interpretation of single-arm trials challenging.
    • Caution is needed because limited data is available on the effect of these interventions on antitumor efficacy, and patients at lower risk typically do not need these interventions.
