# Sideroblastic anemia

* Defective heme biosynthesis within RBC precursors

* Etiologies: hereditary/X-linked (ALAS2 mutations), idiopathic, MDS-RARS, reversible (alcohol, lead, isoniazid, chloramphenicol, copper deficiency, hypothermia)

* Special clinical manifestations: hepatosplenomegaly, iron overload syndromes

* Dx: social, work, & TB hx; can be micro-, normo-, or macrocytic; variable populations of hypochromic RBCs; ↑ Fe, nl TIBC, ↑ ferritin, basophilic stippling, RBC Pappenheimer bodies (Fe-containing inclusions), ring sideroblasts (w/ iron-laden mitochondria) in BM

* Treatment: treat reversible causes; trial of pyridoxine, supportive transfusions for severe anemia with chelation therapy; high-dose pyridoxine for some hereditary cases

Sideroblastic anemia is a type of anemia characterized by defective heme biosynthesis within erythrocyte (red blood cell) precursors. This results in a reduced number of functioning red blood cells. The most common cause of sideroblastic anemia is hereditary, meaning it is passed down in families. However, it can also be caused by idiopathic reasons (unknown cause), myelodysplastic syndrome-related anemia, or be reversible due to factors such as alcohol consumption, lead exposure, isoniazid use, or chloramphenicol use.

There are several special clinical manifestations associated with sideroblastic anemia, including hepatosplenomegaly (enlargement of the liver and spleen) and iron overload syndromes.

Sideroblastic anemia is diagnosed based on a patient's social, work, and tuberculosis history; a physical examination; and laboratory tests. Laboratory tests may show that the patient is microcytic, normocytic, or macrocytic (anemia types based on red blood cell size) and that there is a variable population of hypochromic red blood cells. In addition, iron levels are usually elevated, while total iron-binding capacity is normal. Ferritin levels (a protein that stores iron) are also often elevated, and there may be basophilic stippling (tiny dots on red blood cells) and RBC Pappenheimer bodies (Fe-containing inclusions) on a blood smear. Bone marrow biopsy may reveal ring sideroblasts (cells with iron-laden mitochondria).

Treatment for sideroblastic anemia focuses on treating the underlying cause, if possible. For example, if the anemia is reversible due to alcohol consumption, the patient will be advised to stop drinking. If the cause is unknown, supportive measures such as transfusions and chelation therapy may be used to treat the anemia. In some cases of hereditary sideroblastic anemia, high-dose pyridoxine may be used.
