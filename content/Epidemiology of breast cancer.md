# Epidemiology of breast cancer

## Epidemiology

* In U.S., most common cancer in women; 2nd leading cause of cancer death in women
* Genetic risk: 15–20% ⊕ FHx → 2× ↑ risk; ~45% familial cases a/w germline mutation.
BRCA1/2: 35–85% lifetime risk of breast ca. Germline loss-of-function mutations in PALB2 a/w 35% ↑ risk breast cancer by age 70. Moderate risk mutations CHEK2 and BARD1 a/w 15–30% lifetime risk of breast cancer (NEJM 2021;384:428).

* Estrogen: ↑ risk with early menarche, late menopause, **late parity or nulliparity** (NEJM 2006;354:270); ↑ risk with prolonged HRT (RR = 1.24 after 5.6 y; JAMA 2003;289:3243); OCP use a/w extremely low to no ↑ risk (NEJM 2017:317:2228; JAMA Oncol 2018;4:516)

* The most common cancer in women in the U.S. is breast cancer, and it is the second leading cause of cancer death in women. The genetic risk for breast cancer is **15–20%.** A family history of breast cancer (FHx) is associated with a two-fold increased risk for breast cancer. Approximately 45% of familial cases of breast cancer are associated with a germline mutation. **BRCA1 and BRCA2 mutations are associated with a 35–85%** lifetime risk of breast cancer. Germline loss-of-function mutations in PALB2 are associated with a 35% increased risk of breast cancer by age 70. Moderate risk mutations in CHEK2 and BARD1 are associated with a 15–30% lifetime risk of breast cancer.

* Estrogen is a risk factor for breast cancer. Early menarche, late menopause, late parity, or nulliparity are associated with an increased risk for breast cancer. Prolonged hormone replacement therapy is associated with an increased risk for breast cancer (RR = 1.24 after 5.6 years). Oral contraceptive use is associated with an extremely low to no increased risk for breast cancer.

* Benign breast conditions are associated with an increased risk for breast cancer if they are associated with atypia (atypical ductal or lobular hyperplasia) or proliferative (ductal hyperplasia, papilloma, radial scar, or sclerosing adenosis) features. There is no increased risk for breast cancer associated with cysts, simple fibroadenoma, or columnar changes.

* There is an increased risk for breast cancer associated with a history of **ionizing radiation to the chest** for the treatment of **Hodgkin lymphoma**.

* Modifiable risk factors for breast cancer include obesity (increased risk pre- and postmenopausal), alcohol consumption (greater than three drinks per day associated with a 20% increased risk), and physical inactivity.

* Screening for breast cancer includes mammography every 1–2 years starting at age 50 years, and digital breast tomosynthesis or MRI in high-risk women.
