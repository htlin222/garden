# Treatment and prognosis-Aplastic anemia

* Immunosuppression (CsA/tacrolimus, ATG): 70–80% respond, with 80–90% 5-y survival in responders (96% vs. 76% w/ horse vs. rabbit ATG; NEJM 2011;365:430); 15–20% 10-y incidence of clonal disorders (mostly MDS, AML, PNH)

* TPO mimetics (eg, eltrombopag): use 1st-line w/ immunosuppression (NEJM 2022;386:11)

* Supportive care: transfusions, abx, possible utility of G-CSF & Epo (if Epo <500)

* Allogeneic HSCT: for young Pts → ~80% long-term survival and significantly ↓ risk of malignant evolution, but has risk of transplant-related morbidity & mortality; if possible, avoid transfusions (risk of alloimmunization) pretransplant

The first line of treatment for aplastic anemia is immunosuppression with drugs like cyclosporine and tacrolimus. ATG (anti-thymocyte globulin) is also used in some cases. This treatment results in a 70-80% response rate, with 80-90% five year survival in responders. However, there is a 15-20% ten year incidence of clonal disorders (mostly MDS, AML, PNH) in patients who receive this treatment.

The second line of treatment is TPO mimetics, such as eltrombopag. These drugs are used in conjunction with immunosuppression. This treatment has been shown to be effective in a small number of patients.

Supportive care, including transfusions, antibiotics, and possibly G-CSF (granulocyte colony-stimulating factor) and Epo (erythropoietin), is also important for patients with aplastic anemia. If possible, patients should avoid transfusions before allogeneic HSCT (hematopoietic stem cell transplantation), as transfusions can increase the risk of alloimmunization.

Allogeneic HSCT is the most effective treatment for young patients with aplastic anemia. This treatment results in long-term survival in 80% of patients and significantly decreases the risk of malignant evolution. However, allogeneic HSCT also has a risk of transplant-related morbidity and mortality.
