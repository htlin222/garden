# Cardioversion

## [[consider cardioversion if]]
* If AF >48 h **2–5% risk stroke w/** cardioversion (pharmacologic or electric) ∴ either TEE to r/o thrombus or ensure therapeutic anticoagulation ≥3 wk prior
* If needs to cardiovert urgently, often anticoagulate acutely (eg, IV UFH)

## For AF <36 hrs, 
* no Δ in % in SR at 4 wks w/ early cardioversion vs. wait & see (βb + a/c), with spont cardioversion in 69% and cardioversion required in 28% (NEJM 2019;380:1499)
* Likelihood of success ∝ AF duration & atrial size; control precipitants (eg, vol status, thyroid)
* Before electrical cardiovert, consider pre- Rx w/ AAD (eg, ibutilide), esp. if 1st cardiovert failed
* For pharmacologic cardioversion, class III and IC drugs have best proven efficacy
* If SR returns (spont. or w/ Rx), atria may be mech. stunned; also, high risk of recurrent AF over next 3 mo. ∴ Anticoag postcardioversion ≥4 wk (? unless AF <48 h and low risk).


## Explain by ChatGPT

- Cardioversion is considered if a person has atrial fibrillation, symptoms, tachycardia-mediated cardiomyopathy, or if it is difficult to rate control the heart.
- If cardioversion is needed urgently, anticoagulation is often done acutely.
- The likelihood of success for cardioversion increases if it is done early, the atria are not too large, and if there are no precipitants.
- Before electrical cardioversion, consider pre-treatment with antiarrhythmic drugs, especially if the first cardioversion failed.
- For pharmacologic cardioversion, class III and IC drugs have the best proven efficacy.
- If the heart returns to sinus rhythm (spontaneously or with treatment), the atria may be mechanically stunned. There is also a high risk of recurrent atrial fibrillation over the next three months, so **anticoagulation is usually recommended for at least four weeks** after cardioversion.
