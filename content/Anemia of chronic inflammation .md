# Anemia of chronic inflammation

(ACI; NEJM 2012;366:4)

* ↓ RBC production due to impaired iron utilization and functional iron deficiency from ↑ hepcidin; cytokines (IL-6, TNF-α) cause ↓ Epo responsiveness/production

* Etiologies:
	1. autoimmune disorders,
	2. chronic infection,
	3. inflammation,
	4. HIV,
	5. malignancy

* Dx: ↓ Fe, ↓ TIBC (usually normal or low transferrin sat), ± ↑ ferritin; usually normochromic, normocytic (~70% of cases) but can be microcytic if prolonged

* Coexisting iron deficiency common. Dx clues include ↓ serum ferritin levels, absence of iron staining on BM bx, ⊕ response to a trial of oral iron and/or ↑ soluble transferrin receptor/ferritin index (Am J Clin Pathol 2012;138:642).

Anemia of chronic inflammation is a condition where there is a decrease in red blood cell production due to impaired iron utilization and functional iron deficiency from increased hepcidin. Cytokines (IL-6, TNF-α) cause decreased erythropoietin responsiveness/production.

The etiologies of this condition include:
1. autoimmune disorders,
2. chronic infection,
3. inflammation,
4. HIV,
5. malignancy

The diagnosis of anemia of chronic inflammation is made by decreased iron levels, decreased total iron binding capacity (usually normal or low transferrin saturation), and increased ferritin. The red blood cells are usually normochromic and normocytic (about 70% of cases) but can be microcytic if the condition is prolonged.

Coexisting iron deficiency is common. Diagnostic clues that iron deficiency is present include decreased serum ferritin levels, absence of iron staining on bone marrow biopsy, positive response to a trial of oral iron, and/or increased soluble transferrin receptor/ferritin index.

Treatment of anemia of chronic inflammation includes:
1. Treat the underlying disorder
2. Erythropoiesis-stimulating agents
3. Intravenous iron
